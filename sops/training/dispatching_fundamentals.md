## MODULE 1 — Goals & Career Paths

### Two Paths for Dispatchers
1. **Employed Dispatcher** — Work for a company, fixed salary, set hours, benefits. Lower upside, lower risk.
2. **Self-Employed / Independent Dispatcher** — Work with carriers directly, earn 3–6% commission per load, unlimited earning potential, full autonomy.

### Income Expectations
- Part-time (5–10 trucks): $2,000–$5,000/month
- Full-time (10–20 trucks): $5,000–$15,000/month
- Scale (20+ trucks): $15,000–$40,000+/month
- Key lever: more trucks under management = more revenue

### What Makes a Great Dispatcher
- Relentless on the phone — negotiating, building broker relationships
- Organized — tracks every load, every truck, every document
- Proactive — finds loads before the truck goes empty
- Problem solver — resolves issues without owner involvement

## MODULE 2 — Industry Overview

### The Dispatcher's Role
A dispatcher is the logistics coordinator between:
- **Carriers** (truck owners/drivers) — they move the freight
- **Shippers** (companies with freight) — they need things moved
- **Brokers** (middlemen) — they connect shippers and carriers, take a cut
- **Receivers** (delivery destinations) — warehouses, retailers, distributors

Dispatchers sit between carriers and brokers/shippers. Their job: find the best-paying loads for their carriers.

### How Money Flows
```
Shipper → pays Broker → pays Carrier → Dispatcher earns % of Carrier's rate
```
Example: Shipper pays broker $3,000. Broker pays carrier $2,200. Dispatcher earns 5% = $110 per load.

### Key Stakeholders
| Party | Role | How They Interact With Dispatch |
|---|---|---|
| Shipper | Owns the freight | May book directly (direct contracts) or through broker |
| Broker | Intermediary | Dispatchers call/email brokers to book loads for carriers |
| Carrier | Truck owner/operator | Dispatcher's primary client — the one who pays commission |
| Receiver | Delivery destination | Confirms delivery, signs BOL |
| Factoring Company | Finances receivables | Pays carrier quickly; collects from broker 30–90 days |

## MODULE 4 — Critical Documentation

### Carrier Setup Documents (Required Before First Load)
Every carrier must provide these before Erin books their first load:

| Document | What It Is | Who Issues It |
|---|---|---|
| MC Certificate | Operating authority from FMCSA | FMCSA (fmcsa.dot.gov) |
| COI (Certificate of Insurance) | Proof of cargo + liability insurance | Carrier's insurance agent |
| W-9 | Tax ID form for payments | Carrier fills out |
| NOA (Notice of Assignment) | Directs broker to pay factoring company | Factoring company issues |
| Carrier Agreement | Contract between carrier and dispatch | Dispatch generates |

### Per-Load Documents
| Document | When Created | Who Creates |
|---|---|---|
| Rate Confirmation | Before pickup | Broker sends to carrier |
| Bill of Lading (BOL) | At pickup | Shipper creates, driver carries |
| Proof of Delivery (POD) | At delivery | Driver gets from receiver |
| Invoice | After delivery | Carrier/Dispatch sends to broker |

### Compliance Notes (Erin + Compliance Agent)
- **Rate Conf must be signed before any truck moves**
- **COI must name the broker as additional insured** on many loads
- **Factoring: NOA directs payment to factoring company** — broker must have NOA on file

## MODULE 5 — 9-Step Dispatch Workflow (Erin's Core Process)

### Step 1: Planning
- Know the truck's location, availability date/time
- Know the truck's home region and preferred lanes
- Know the carrier's equipment specs (length, weight capacity, lift gate, etc.)
- Check hours of service — how many hours has the driver driven this week?

### Step 2: Post the Truck
- Post on load boards: DAT, Truck Stop, 123LoadBoard, Doft
- Post 1–2 days in advance of availability
- Include: equipment type, location, available date, preferred destination

### Step 3: Search for Loads
- Search load boards simultaneously
- Filter by: pickup location radius, destination, equipment type, date
- Use lane analysis to find high-demand corridors
- Target loads that position truck well for the next move

### Step 4: Evaluate & Select Loads
**4 Load Selection Approaches:**
1. **Best RPM** — Highest revenue per mile wins
2. **Best Lane Positioning** — Positions truck for even better next load
3. **Best Relationship** — Preferred broker who pays fast and books reliably
4. **Deadhead Minimization** — Shortest empty miles to pickup

**Always calculate:**
- Gross revenue (what broker pays carrier)
- Deadhead miles (empty miles to pickup)
- Net RPM = Gross ÷ (loaded miles + deadhead miles)

### Step 5: Book the Load
- Call or email broker
- Get verbal agreement first
- Broker sends Rate Confirmation
- Carrier (or dispatcher on their behalf) signs Rate Confirmation
- Never let a truck move without a signed Rate Conf

### Step 6: Coordinate Pickup
- Confirm pickup appointment with shipper (if scheduled)
- Give driver: pickup address, contact name, appointment time, load reference number
- Verify driver has BOL instructions
- Check in with driver at pickup — confirm loaded, note actual weight

### Step 7: Transit Monitoring
- Check in every 4–6 hours on long hauls
- Know where the truck is at all times
- Watch for weather, traffic, breakdowns
- Communicate proactively with broker if delays expected
- **Never surprise a broker — always call ahead if late**

### Step 8: Delivery
- Confirm delivery appointment time
- Driver gets BOL signed by receiver (Proof of Delivery = POD)
- Driver sends/photos POD to dispatcher immediately
- Dispatcher confirms with broker that delivery is complete

### Step 9: Invoicing & Record Keeping
- Submit invoice + signed Rate Conf + POD to broker (or factoring company)
- Standard payment: net 30–45 days (factoring: same day)
- Log load in tracking system: dates, rates, miles, broker contact, rating
- Update carrier earnings record

## MODULE 6 — Hours of Service (HOS) Regulations

### The Rules (FMCSA — USA)
| Rule | Limit |
|---|---|
| 14-Hour Window | Driver has 14 hours from first on-duty moment to complete driving |
| 11-Hour Driving | Max 11 hours of actual driving within the 14-hr window |
| 30-Minute Break | Required after 8 hours of driving (can be off-duty or sleeper) |
| 70-Hour/8-Day | Max 70 hours on-duty in any 8-day period |
| 34-Hour Reset | Off-duty for 34 consecutive hours resets the 70-hr clock |
| 10-Hour Off-Duty | Must take 10 hours off before next on-duty period |

### ELD (Electronic Logging Device)
- Required for most CDL drivers
- Automatically records driving time
- **Erin must know driver's available hours before assigning any load**
- Never assign a load that would require driving beyond legal limits

### Dispatch Responsibility
- Dispatchers are NOT allowed to pressure drivers to violate HOS
- If a driver says they're out of hours — accept it, find another solution
- **Erin: always confirm hours available before quoting delivery windows to brokers**

## MODULE 7 — Market Analysis

### Understanding Freight Flows
- Freight moves from manufacturing centers → population centers
- Heavy outbound lanes: Midwest (manufacturing), Texas (energy/ag), Southeast (poultry/produce)
- Heavy inbound lanes: Northeast (high demand, premium rates), California (imports, retail)
- Seasonal: Midwest loads spike in harvest (Sep–Nov); produce peaks spring/summer

### Tools for Market Analysis
| Tool | What It Shows |
|---|---|
| DAT TrendLines | Historical rate data by lane, market rates vs. your rates |
| 123LoadBoard | Live load board + rate history |
| Truck Stop (Truckstop.io) | Load board + broker credit scores |
| FreightWaves | Industry news, market conditions |
| FMCSA | Carrier/broker registration, authority verification |

### Load Board Strategy
- **DAT** — Largest network, most loads, industry standard
- **123LoadBoard** — Good rates, solid broker database, lower cost
- **Doft** — Free tier, good for box trucks and smaller carriers
- **Truck Stop** — Strong broker relationships, good for flatbed too

### Lane Analysis Process
1. Search your carrier's home market
2. Look at available loads vs. trucks (demand/supply ratio)
3. Find lanes where loads outnumber trucks — rates go up
4. Avoid lanes with too many trucks competing — rates get bid down
5. Target lanes that end near the carrier's home or next desired market

## MODULE 8 — Negotiation

### Core Principle
Brokers post low. You counter high. Both sides expect negotiation.

### Negotiation Script — Inbound Broker Call
```
Broker: "I have a load going from Chicago to Dallas, 892 miles, dry van. Paying $2,800."
Dispatcher: "What's the best you can do on that? We're seeing $3.25 on that lane right now."
Broker: "I can go to $2,950."
Dispatcher: "I need $3,100 to make it work for my carrier. Can you get there?"
Broker: "Let me check... I can do $3,050."
Dispatcher: "Done. Send me the rate confirmation."
```

### Key Rules for Negotiation
1. **Always ask for more than you expect** — leave room to meet in the middle
2. **Never accept the first offer** — brokers expect a counter
3. **Use lane data** — "DAT shows $3.20 on this lane" is leverage
4. **Know your floor** — Erin's floor is $2.75/RPM (Iron Rule). Never go below.
5. **Time creates urgency** — "I have another option I need to respond to" is real pressure
6. **Build relationships** — brokers who like you give better loads before posting publicly

### When to Walk Away
- Rate is below $2.75/RPM after negotiation → reject (Iron Rule)
- Broker has poor credit score (check Truck Stop broker credit) → reject
- Carrier load matches but deadhead exceeds limits → reject (Iron Rule)

## MODULE 9 — Building Broker Relationships

### Why Relationships Matter
- Top brokers give dispatchers first call on premium loads before posting
- Repeat business = faster booking, less negotiation time
- Reliable dispatchers get better treatment during tight markets

### How to Build Relationships
1. **Deliver on your promises** — if you say the truck will be there at 2pm, be there at 2pm
2. **Communicate proactively** — never let a broker find out about a problem from the driver
3. **Follow up after delivery** — quick email: "Load delivered, BOL attached. Looking for more Chicago→Dallas lanes."
4. **Track broker performance** — note who pays fast, who has good loads, who is difficult
5. **Send loads back** — if you can't take a load, refer the broker to a trustworthy carrier. They remember.

### Red Flags — Brokers to Avoid
- Credit score below 70 on Truck Stop
- Slow pay history (net 60+ with disputes)
- Pressures drivers to violate HOS
- Lowballs every single load, no relationship value
- Won't send rate confirmation before truck moves

## MODULE 10 — Problem Solving & Conflict Resolution

### Common Problems and Responses

**Truck broke down in transit:**
1. Driver notifies dispatcher immediately
2. Dispatcher calls broker immediately — don't wait, don't hide it
3. Dispatcher arranges rescue truck or relay (broker may help)
4. Document everything — photos, timestamps, towing records
5. Notify shipper/receiver if delivery will be delayed

**Load refused at delivery (damaged goods claim):**
1. Driver gets exception noted on BOL before signing
2. Photos at delivery — all sides, all damage
3. Driver does NOT argue at receiver — gets signature with exception, leaves
4. Dispatcher contacts broker with photos and BOL within 2 hours
5. Carrier's insurance handles claim from there

**Broker won't pay:**
1. Send invoice + POD + Rate Conf via email (paper trail)
2. 30 days: reminder email
3. 45 days: call broker's accounts payable directly
4. 60 days: file complaint with FMCSA (broker bond claim)
5. Factoring companies handle collections — another reason to factor

**Driver won't pick up a load:**
1. Find out why — HOS issue? Home time? Safety concern?
2. If legitimate, find substitute truck or notify broker immediately
3. If no reason: have a clear carrier agreement that specifies obligations
4. Last resort: never use that carrier again, document in records

**Rate confirmation dispute (broker won't honor rate):**
1. Pull up signed rate confirmation — you have it, they signed it
2. Firm and calm: "We have a signed rate confirmation for $X. I need payment as agreed."
3. If they refuse: FMCSA broker bond claim ($75K bond required by law)
4. Document every call — date, time, who you spoke to, what they said

## MODULE 11 — Sales: Getting Carriers as Clients

### Cold Calling — Getting Dispatcher Clients
Dispatchers need to find carriers who need help. This is a sales role.

**Where to Find Carriers:**
- FMCSA's SAFER database — search active MC numbers in your region
- Facebook groups — "Owner Operators" groups, truck drivers groups
- Trucking Facebook Marketplace — carriers often post about needing dispatchers
- Load boards — carriers post their trucks, contact them directly
- Truck stops — yes, in person works

**Cold Call Script:**
```
"Hi, is this [Name]? Great — I found your truck on [source]. My name is [Name], 
I'm a freight dispatcher. I help owner-operators like you find consistent loads 
at $3.00+ per mile without the headache of calling brokers all day. 

Do you currently work with a dispatcher? [No] Perfect — can I take 5 minutes 
to show you what I can do for you?

[Yes] Who handles your dispatching? And are you happy with the rates you're getting?
If they're not getting $3.00+ consistently, I can probably do better. Want to compare?"
```

**Objections and Responses:**
- "I dispatch myself" → "How many hours a week do you spend on it? I can take that off your plate so you just drive."
- "I can't afford a dispatcher" → "I work on commission — I only make money when you make money. No load, no fee."
- "I had a bad dispatcher before" → "Tell me what went wrong. Here's how I'm different: [specific difference]."
- "I need to think about it" → "Of course. Can I follow up Thursday? What's the best time?"

### Email Marketing to Carriers
**Subject line options:**
- "Getting $3.00+/mile on your Chicago runs?"
- "Free dispatch consultation for owner-operators"
- "I found 3 loads this week you'd love"

**Email body:**
- Short (under 150 words)
- One clear value proposition: better rates, less hassle
- One CTA: "Reply to this email" or "Call me at [number]"
- No attachments on first email

### Social Media Lead Gen (Facebook)
1. Join owner-operator and trucking Facebook groups
2. Post value: "Here are the top-paying lanes out of Chicago this week..." (not a sales pitch)
3. When carriers engage, DM them: "Hey [name], saw you're based in Chicago — I work with a few OOs there. Want to see what I'm getting for them?"
4. Build a Facebook page: post rate updates, trucking tips, success stories

## MODULE 12 — Pricing Your Dispatch Services

### Standard Commission Rates
| Carrier Relationship | Commission Rate |
|---|---|
| New carrier (first 90 days) | 5–6% of gross carrier rate |
| Established carrier (90+ days) | 3–5% of gross carrier rate |
| High-volume carrier (10+ loads/mo) | 3% (negotiate for volume) |
| Flat fee option | $200–$350/truck/week |

### AI Dispatch Rates (Iron Rules — from .env)
```
COMMISSION_RATE_NEW = 0.10 (10% — new carriers first 90 days)
COMMISSION_RATE_EXISTING = 0.08 (8% — established carriers)
```

### What's Included in Service
- Load searching on all major boards
- Negotiation with brokers
- Rate confirmation review and signing
- Transit monitoring
- Delivery confirmation
- Invoice submission
- 24/7 availability for issues

### Carrier Agreement Must Include
1. Commission rate and how it's calculated
2. Payment terms (weekly, bi-weekly)
3. Minimum notice period to terminate
4. What happens if carrier refuses a load
5. Liability limitations
6. How disputes are handled
