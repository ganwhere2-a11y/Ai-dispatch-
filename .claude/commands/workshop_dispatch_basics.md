# Workshop: Dispatch Basics — How Freight Actually Works

## What You'll Learn
What a freight dispatcher does, how loads move from shipper to carrier, and how you make money on every single load. By the end you'll be able to explain the whole business in 60 seconds.

---

## The Lesson

**The 3 players in every freight transaction:**

1. **The Shipper** — The company that has stuff to move. A hospital that needs surgical supplies delivered. A pharma company shipping medication to a clinic. They have freight and they need someone to move it.

2. **The Carrier** — The truck driver or trucking company that physically moves the freight. They have the truck. They need loads to haul so they make money.

3. **You — The Dispatcher** — You sit in the middle. You find loads for carriers and find carriers for shippers. You make everything match up. You get paid a percentage of the load rate for making it happen.

**How the money works:**

```
Shipper pays: $3,000 for a load
Your cut (8%): $240
Carrier gets: $2,760
```

You never touch the freight. You never drive a truck. You make money by being the person who knows which carrier can run which lane, at what price, and when.

**The difference between a Broker and a Dispatcher:**
- A **freight broker** works for the shipper. They find carriers for shippers.
- A **dispatcher** works for the carrier. They find loads for carriers.
- You're technically operating as a dispatcher service — your client is the carrier, but you build shipper relationships too.

**What Erin does all day:**
- Checks load boards (DAT, Truckstop) for available loads
- Runs the profit formula on every load she finds
- Matches loads to the right carrier based on location, equipment, lane history
- Sends rate confirmations and books the load
- Generates all paperwork (BOL, carrier packet)
- Tracks the shipment until delivery
- Submits the invoice

**The Load Lifecycle (8 steps):**
1. Load request comes in (shipper calls or emails)
2. Compliance checks the load (is it legal to haul?)
3. Erin scores the load (does it make money?)
4. Erin finds the best available carrier
5. Rate is calculated and confirmed
6. Documents are generated
7. Carrier picks up and delivers
8. Invoice is sent and collected

## Practice Questions

**Question 1:** A shipper wants to move medical supplies from Chicago to Indianapolis. The carrier quotes $1,800 for the load. You charge 8%. How much do you make? How much does the carrier get?

*Expected: You make $144. Carrier gets $1,656.*

**Question 2:** What's the difference between "loaded miles" and "deadhead miles"?

*Expected: Loaded miles = miles driven with freight on the truck. Deadhead miles = miles driven with an empty truck to pick up the next load. Deadhead costs money but generates no revenue.*

**Question 3:** Why does it matter that Erin works FOR the carrier, not the shipper?

*Expected: It shapes the relationship. Erin's job is to find the carrier profitable loads. The carrier trusts Erin to protect their interests. This is different from a broker who negotiates against the carrier to get the cheapest rate.*

**Question 4:** Name 3 things that could make a load go wrong.

*Expected: Carrier breaks down, load rejected at delivery, wrong paperwork, shipper not ready at pickup, carrier cancels last minute, weather delays, weight overload.*

## Mastery Check

*Expected answers:*
*1. $1,638 (780 × $2.10)*

*3. $131*
*4. Yes — time-sensitive means Erin prioritizes carrier reliability over price, compliance is double-checked, and Maya is notified*
