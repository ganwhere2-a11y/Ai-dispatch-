# Workshop: EU Compliance — Medical Freight in Europe

## What You'll Learn
What GDP (Good Distribution Practice) requires for medical freight in the EU, what CMR documents are, the cabotage rules that limit what a non-EU carrier can do, and why EU expansion requires careful planning before the first load.

---

## The Lesson

**This workshop is forward-looking — EU is Phase 3 (Month 12+).**
EU expansion requires owner approval (Tier 3). This workshop prepares you to understand what's required when that day comes.

**What is EU GDP?**
GDP stands for Good Distribution Practice — the EU's guidelines for the distribution of medicinal products for human use. Issued by the European Commission. Applies to any party in the supply chain of pharmaceutical products, not just manufacturers.

**What GDP requires for freight:**
- Temperature monitoring during transport (even for non-temperature-sensitive products — you document that temp was maintained)
- Chain-of-custody documentation — who handled the product at every step
- Carrier qualification — the transport company must be GDP-compliant (audited)
- Risk assessment for every shipment
- Complaint handling and recall procedures

**This means:** You can't just dispatch any carrier for pharma loads in Europe. The carrier must be GDP-qualified. That's a whole additional vetting step beyond FMCSA-equivalent checks.

**CMR Document (Convention on the Contract for the International Carriage of Goods by Road)**
Every road freight shipment in Europe requires a CMR waybill. It's the EU equivalent of a US Bill of Lading, but standardized across all EU member states. It covers:
- Shipper, carrier, consignee details
- Description of goods
- Place and date of taking over goods
- Place designated for delivery
- Carrier liability terms

**Cabotage Rules — Critical for US Carriers**
Cabotage = carrying goods within a country you're not based in.

EU rules: A non-EU carrier can enter the EU to make a single delivery (from outside the EU), then make **3 cabotage operations within 7 days** before they must leave. After that, the truck must return to its home country.

This means: A US carrier cannot set up permanent EU freight operations from the US. You need either a EU-based carrier partner OR establish a EU entity.

**Per-Country Notes:**
- **Germany**: Strict enforcement of EU regulations. Strong pharma distribution market.
- **Netherlands**: Major hub (Rotterdam port). GDP compliance heavily enforced for pharma.
- **France**: Requires French transport license (LICT) for road haulage within France.
- **UK (Post-Brexit)**: No longer in EU — separate rules apply. UK has its own GDP guidelines (MHRA). UK-EU freight now requires customs clearance on both sides.

## Practice Questions

**Question 1:** Why does EU expansion require Tier 3 owner approval, not just a Tier 2 flag to Maya?

*Expected: EU expansion is a major business decision — new regulatory framework, potential need for an EU legal entity, GDP carrier qualification process, CMR documentation setup, and new liability exposure. It's not just "find a new lane." It requires the owner to intentionally authorize this market entry.*

**Question 2:** A US carrier with Satisfactory FMCSA rating wants to haul pharma in Germany. What additional qualification do they need?

*Expected: GDP compliance certification. They need to be audited against EU GDP guidelines — this is separate from and in addition to FMCSA. Without it, they cannot legally transport medicinal products in the EU under GDP requirements.*

**Question 3:** What's the difference between a CMR and a US Bill of Lading?

*Expected: A BOL is a US document, format varies by carrier/shipper. A CMR is a standardized legal document used across all EU member states under an international treaty. It sets out the terms of the freight contract and carrier liability. Both serve similar purposes (evidence of contract, receipt of goods) but the CMR is legally standardized and internationally recognized.*
